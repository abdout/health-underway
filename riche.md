# Café Riche: A Historic Egyptian Landmark

## Overview

**Café Riche** (Arabic: مقهى ريش) is one of the most renowned landmarks in downtown Cairo, Egypt. Located at 17th Talaat Harb Street, this historic café has served as a meeting place for intellectuals, revolutionaries, and cultural figures for over a century.

## Historical Timeline

### Early Years (1908-1914)
- **1908**: Café established (original founding date)
- **1914**: Renamed "Café Riche" when purchased by French businessman Henry Recine
- Shortly after acquisition, Recine sold the café to Michael Nicoapolits from Greece and returned to France

### Greek Ownership Era (1914-1962)
- **Michael Nicoapolits** added theatrical performances to the café
- Featured legendary performers including:
  - Monira il-Mahdiyya
  - Umm Kulthum
- **November 4, 1942**: Nicoapolits sold the café to George Basile Avayianos
- Avayianos focused on adding restaurant services to complement the café

### Egyptian Ownership (1962-Present)
- **1962**: Abdel Malek Michael Salib became the first native Egyptian owner
- This ownership change marked Egypt's broader movement to reclaim economic identity from foreign control
- **2015**: Manager and co-owner Magdy Abdel-Malak passed away, leaving the café's future uncertain

## Cultural & Political Significance

### Revolutionary Hub
- **1919 Revolution**: Revolutionaries met in the basement to organize activities and print flyers against British rule
- **December 19, 1919**: Site of a failed assassination attempt on Egypt's last Coptic Prime Minister, Youssef Wahba Pacha
- **1952 Revolution**: Gamal Abdel Nasser frequented the café while planning the overthrow of King Farouk

### Literary & Intellectual Center
- **Nobel Laureate Naguib Mahfouz** was a regular patron
  - The café would close on Fridays specifically for Mahfouz's meetings
  - He mentioned the café in several of his books
  - Drew inspiration for characters from fellow customers
- Other notable literary figures included:
  - Salah Jaheen (poet)
  - Amal Donkul (poet)
  - Ibrahim Abdel-Meguid (novelist) described it as "the place where Egypt's modern literary trends are set"

### Royal Connections
- King Farouk reportedly first saw his second wife, Nariman Sadek, at the café

## Physical Description & Atmosphere

### Interior Design
- Warm wooden interiors
- Walls adorned with framed photos of Egypt's iconic celebrities
- Small outdoor theatre in early days where Om Kalthoum and Sayed Darwish performed

### Clientele Culture
- Exclusive atmosphere - mainly served regulars from cultural and intellectual circles
- Non-regulars often required to order main courses or leave
- Prime location near Soliman-Pasha Square (now Talaat Harb Square) and Tahrir Square

## Challenges & Modern Era

### Decline Period (1990s)
- Closed for almost a decade due to:
  - Legal disputes with Egyptian government over public passage occupation
  - Damage from October 1992 earthquake
  - Rise of digital media reducing formal meeting place culture
  - Competition from shopping malls affecting small businesses

### Recent Revival
- **2011 Revolution**: Served as refuge for protesters during the Egyptian Revolution
- Continued to maintain its role as a cultural gathering place despite modern challenges

## Legacy & Impact

### Cultural Influence
- Witness to Egypt's major political upheavals across the 20th century
- Hub for intellectual discourse and literary development
- Symbol of Egypt's transition from foreign to native ownership
- Inspiration for numerous literary works and cultural references

### Historical Significance
- Over 100 years of continuous operation (with brief closure periods)
- Documented meeting place for multiple generations of Egyptian leaders
- Preserved example of early 20th-century café culture in the Middle East
